# Beyond phytoplankton and phosphorus: assessing trophic state in freshwater benthic ecosystems


## Abstract

Phytoplankton biomass and water-column total phosphorus (TP) concentrations are reliable indicators of trophic status in lakes where most P resides in planktonic biomass. However, these sestonic metrics lose effectiveness in P-limited lentic systems dominated by benthic microbial and macrophyte producers. Despite this limitation, they continue to be used to assess eutrophication in benthic ecosystems, largely in the absence of more appropriate tools. We conducted a literature review to characterize the strength and forms of relationship between water-column TP and biotic TP, and the biomass of phytoplankton, benthic algae, and macrophytes across P-limited lentic benthic ecosystems. As expected, phytoplankton and benthic algal biomass increased with increasing water-column TP in littoral zones and shallow lakes and ponds. In contrast, benthic algal biomass in wetlands was weakly negatively correlated with water-column TP but strongly positively correlated with benthic microbial TP standing stock (g m-3); this positive relationship also occurred in all other benthic ecosystems. To reconcile these contrasting patterns, we evaluated benthic microbial and macrophyte biomass along a TP gradient in two karstic wetlands, where benthic algal biomass declines with increasing water-column TP. In these systems, microbial and macrophyte biomass increased with TP standing stock (g m-3) of both producer groups. A benthic standing-stock approach for assessing trophic status in wetlands is therefore comparable to sestonic approaches in lakes, requires similar effort, and more accurately reflects ecosystem production capacity.

## Manuscript Highlights

- Water-column phosphorus is not a reliable indicator of chlorophyll-a production in p-limited freshwater benthic ecosystems.
- Instead, benthic producer phosphorus standing stock forms a predicable positive relationship with benthic chlorophyll-a. 
- This relationship can be used to develop a trophic status classification system for shallow lentic systems like wetlands.
